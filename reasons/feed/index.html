]]> That’s a pretty good reason to love my wife. She’s coming home today! In fact, right now, she’s somewhere over the ocean near Alaska. 35,000 feet above the earth, probably watching an in-flight movie or taking a half-nap. In four hours or so, I’ll see her walk (or more likely run) through SFO and into my arms.

I’m probably being a little over dramatic, but I’m excited. 40 days is a long time to be without someone with whom you’re used to spending every day.

Whenever people hear that she’s been gone for so long, they typically have had one of two reactions. They either say, “oh, that must be hard. Is this the longest you’ve been apart since you got married?” or “oh man – you’re a summer bachelor, are you enjoying that?” I won’t lie and say that the second answer has always been a no. There have been times over the past 40 days when the simplicity of being alone has been rather refreshing. I was able to focus on some personal things that I’ve been wanting to work on, and I think I took good advantage of the time. I think Kate would say the same thing, although I think she’s more glad that I had the time for myself, than that she had it for herself.

It was often odd to respond to the “oh, that must be hard” reaction. Of course it was difficult to adjust to a routine without Kate, and emotionally it was hard to not have her here with me like usual, but then I think of all the other circumstances by which our relationship could be tested and the past 40 days were anything but hard. Kakao talk (a Korean Messaging app) and FaceTime allowed us to talk and usually see each other every day. We exchanged emails and updates every morning and evening. I had this running blog to remind her how much I love her. Similarly, she had an Instagram photo printed for each day that she was gone, wrote a note on each, and I opened one each day before bed (yes, I know – we’re ridiculous). Of course nothing replaces a real hug or face to face conversation, but considering the distance, I think everything went pretty darn well. Kate definitely had a harder time than I did. She was in a foreign place, without very many friends or comforts around her, but she made it and thrived.

So, I usually responded, “yeah, it’s been a challenge, but overall it’s been a good thing for both of us.” In some ways I think Kate and I have gotten closer, or at least clarified what we value in our relationship. In absence, it’s easier to take stock of what is important to you, realize what you value about the other person, and see things that usually fall in the blind spots of daily life. It’s can be a valuable exercise to spend an extended amount of time apart from someone you’re crazy about. I guess, that it’s true that “absence makes the heart grow fonder” (cue: sappy music swells in the background).

All that said, I’m very glad she’s coming home today. The past 40 days have been challenging and valuable, but I’d of course much rather have her here by my side. And soon, she will be!

]]> When Kate was preparing for her trip to Korea, she was worried that she wouldn’t be very good in the Yonsei language program where she would be studying. She had convinced herself that she wasn’t good at learning languages. Sure, the way she processes information may not lend itself well to learning a language, but she clearly is more than capable of doing it. She learned a decent amount of Spanish in high school, became nearly fluent in Italian in college and over the course of the past year became basic conversationally fluent in Korean.

Regardless, she was nervous about the Yonsei program because it’s the “Middlebury of Korean Language”, the “Harvard of Asian Language study.” In other words, it’s one of the best, and it’s really hard. She was told that students who had taken through the fourth level of competing programs were often unable to pass the second level at Yonsei.

Well, Kate passed her program – with all A’s. That’s amazing! She’ll be quick to tell you that ‘it was the first level’ or ‘a lot of it was review for her’ but those things don’t matter and are only partially true. She tested into a certain level, then asked to move up to a higher level that was out of her comfort zone, so she was really in level 1.5 or something like that. It moved faster and went further in the book. Certainly some of it was review, but that is always the case with a language class, and is by design.

I can’t wait to hear about all the things she learned and to hear her speak with her tutor here in the U.S. I think she’ll be very proud of Kate’s progress, as she should be. You see… my wife is super smart. Despite missing me soooo much, she was able to muster the energy to study hard and pass the hardest Korean language program in Korea. Go Kate!
